Full-text quantitative analysis (sentence length distribution, word frequency, punctuation patterns, figurative language density, emotional register) combined with qualitative close reading.
>
> Fitzgerald's prose is the art of making decadence feel like mourning. His sentences are long, sensory, and rhythmically controlled. His narrator is unreliable but deeply self-aware. His imagery encodes moral judgment. His endings are the most famous in American literature — not because they resolve, but because they refuse to.
>
> **Scope limit:** This is primarily a *Great Gatsby* style profile, not a statistically representative profile of Fitzgerald's entire career. Treat Nick Carraway's participant-observer voice and the novel's social world as work-specific options.

## One-Line Definition

Lyrical precision — long, musical sentences saturated with sensory detail and symbolic imagery, narrated by a morally ambivalent first-person observer who judges the world while claiming not to, ending always in elegy.

## 1. Subject Layer (选题层)

| Dimension | Characteristic |
|-----------|---------------|
| Subject type | Class conflict, the American Dream, wealth and moral decay, love as illusion, the 1920s Jazz Age |
| Entry angle | Intimate personal relationships (a neighbor, a cousin, a lover) that open onto vast social and historical questions |
| Core question | "What happens when you build your entire life around a dream that was already dead before you started?" |
| Conflict source | Old money vs. new money, aspiration vs. reality, romantic idealism vs. moral corruption, East vs. Midwest |
| Focus object | Self-made outsiders (Gatsby), careless aristocrats (Tom and Daisy), morally compromised observers (Nick), destroyed innocents (Myrtle, George Wilson) |
| Subject temperament | Elegiac, romantic, disillusioned, bittersweet |

**The Fitzgerald subject rule:** Write about the person who wants something too much, in the company of people who want nothing at all. The collision between yearning and indifference is where the story lives.

> "He talked a lot about the past, and I gathered that he wanted to recover something, some idea of himself perhaps, that had gone into loving Daisy."

---

## 2. Stance Layer (立场层)

| Dimension | Characteristic |
|-----------|---------------|
| Author position | Participant-observer. Inside the world but apart from it. Claims objectivity while deeply implicated. |
| Attitude toward characters | Sympathetic to dreamers, contemptuous of the careless rich, self-aware about his own complicity |
| Attitude toward society | Structural critique wrapped in personal narrative. Not a polemic — an elegy that implies judgment |
| Judgment method | Through detail selection, symbolic placement, and devastating closing lines. Never editorializes in the body; the structure itself delivers the verdict |
| Value foundation | Romantic disillusionment. Believes in the beauty of the dream even while showing its destruction |

**Core rule:** Surface neutrality, buried judgment. The narrator says "I'm inclined to reserve all judgements" — and then the entire novel is a carefully calibrated judgment.

> "They were careless people, Tom and Daisy — they smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their money or their vast carelessness, or whatever it was that kept them together, and let other people clean up the mess they had made."

This sentence — delivered near the end, after hundreds of pages of apparent neutrality — is the novel's moral thesis. It works because it was earned through restraint.

## 3. Structure Layer (结构层)

### Opening Type: Reflective Retrospective Frame

The novel opens with the narrator establishing his own character and limitations before the story begins:

> "In my younger and more vulnerable years my father gave me some advice that I've been turning over in my mind ever since."

This is the **moral frame opening** — the narrator tells you upfront that this is a story about judgment, and about how hard judgment is. It signals: I will be unreliable, but I will try to be honest.

### Mid-Section Structure: Three-Act Revelation

| Act | Chapters | Structure |
|-----|----------|-----------|
| Act I: Setup | I-III | Nick arrives → observes East Egg wealth → attends Gatsby's party → meets Gatsby |
| Act II: Complication | IV-VI | Gatsby's past revealed → reunion with Daisy → the dream seems possible |
| Act III: Destruction | VII-IX | Confrontation → death → aftermath → elegy |

The structure is **peeling the onion**: every chapter strips away another layer of mystery about Gatsby. Information is released in fragments — rumor, then partial truth, then devastating full revelation.

### Ending Type: Elegiac Meditation / Symbolic Universalization

The ending moves from specific (Gatsby's death) to universal (the American Dream):

> "Gatsby believed in the green light, the orgiastic future that year by year recedes before us. It eluded us then, but that's no matter — tomorrow we will run faster, stretch out our arms farther … And one fine morning —
>
> So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past."

**Ending rule:** Move from the particular to the universal. The final image must be a metaphor that encompasses both the character's fate and a larger human condition. End on rhythm, not on explanation.

## 4. Narrative Layer (叙事层)

| Dimension | Characteristic |
|-----------|---------------|
| Narrative POV | First-person retrospective (Nick Carraway). Present at key events, absent for others, reconstructs from imagination and testimony |
| Camera distance | Alternates between close interior (Nick's feelings), mid-range (observed dialogue and gesture), and vast panoramic (landscape as symbol) |
| Time handling | Non-linear. Opens in the present, flashes back to the summer, incorporates Gatsby's backstory through third-party narration |
| Scene density | High. Most paragraphs contain concrete sensory detail — rooms, lawns, weather, clothing, light |
| Character presentation | Primarily through dialogue, gesture, and symbolic appearance. Rarely through direct psychological analysis |
| Information release | Deliberately withheld. Gatsby's true identity, the car accident, Daisy's feelings — all revealed gradually through rumor, accident, and confrontation |
| Narrative pace | Slow and luxurious in description, fast and snappy in dialogue. Long descriptive passages alternate with rapid-fire exchanges |

### The Nick Carraway Technique

Nick is the essential Fitzgerald device: a narrator who is both inside and outside, sympathetic and judgmental, honest and self-deceived. He tells you he reserves judgment, then delivers the most devastating judgments in American literature.

> "Everyone suspects himself of at least one of the cardinal virtues, and this is mine: I am one of the few honest people that I have ever known."

**Narrative rule:** Use a first-person narrator who is implicated in the world they describe. The tension between the narrator's claimed objectivity and their actual bias creates the novel's moral texture.

### Multi-Layer Camera Work

Fitzgerald moves fluidly between three camera distances:

**Panoramic (landscape as moral symbol):**
> "Across the courtesy bay the white palaces of fashionable East Egg glittered along the water."

**Mid-range (social observation):**
> "He had changed since his New Haven years. Now he was a sturdy, straw-haired man of thirty with a rather hard mouth and a supercilious manner."

**Close-up (intimate sensory detail):**
> "The only completely stationary object in the room was an enormous couch on which two young women were buoyed up as though upon an anchored balloon."

## 5. Paragraph Layer (段落层)

| Paragraph Type | Function | Frequency |
|----------------|----------|-----------|
| Scene paragraph | Establishes visual/auditory atmosphere | Very high — most paragraphs ground in sensory detail |
| Dialogue paragraph | Advances plot through speech | High — extended dialogue sequences |
| Reflective paragraph | Nick's moral commentary | Medium — appears at turning points |
| Catalogue paragraph | Lists of names, guests, details to establish social texture | Medium — used for social panorama |
| Symbolic paragraph | A single image or detail loaded with meaning | Medium — appears at structural pivots |
| Transition paragraph | Bridges time or location | Low — Fitzgerald prefers scene-to-scene jumps |

### The Catalogue Paragraph

One of Fitzgerald's signature techniques: long lists of names and social details that create the texture of a world:

> "From East Egg, then, came the Chester Beckers and the Leeches, and a man named Bunsen, whom I knew at Yale, and Doctor Webster Civet, who was drowned last summer up in Maine. And the Hornbeams and the Willie Voltaires, and a whole clan named Blackbuck, who always gathered in a corner and flipped up their noses like goats at whosoever came near."

This works because the names themselves are absurd, and the parenthetical details ("who was drowned last summer," "who flipped up their noses like goats") inject moral commentary into what seems like mere social cataloguing.

### The Symbolic Pivot Paragraph

At key structural moments, a single paragraph carries enormous symbolic weight:

> "The wind had blown off, leaving a loud, bright night on the wings of cold moonlight, and the entrepreneurs and their victims and the jazz musicians and the dancers and the lights and the cars and the voices merged into one."

**Paragraph rule:** Every paragraph must serve at least one of three functions — build atmosphere, advance plot, or deliver judgment. Ideally, all three simultaneously.

### Sentence Architecture

Fitzgerald's sentences have a characteristic architecture: **long, complex, multi-clausal, building toward a final image or judgment that lands like a punch.**

**The Cumulative Sentence (most common pattern):**
Main clause + series of modifying phrases that accumulate detail and meaning:

> "In my younger and more vulnerable years my father gave me some advice that I've been turning over in my mind ever since."

> "If personality is an unbroken series of successful gestures, then there was something gorgeous about him, some heightened sensitivity to the promises of life, as if he were related to one of those intricate machines that register earthquakes ten thousand miles away."

**The Em-Dash Interruption:**
Fitzgerald uses em dashes (417 in the novel) to insert qualifications, reversals, and parenthetical observations:

> "No — Gatsby turned out all right at the end; it is what preyed on Gatsby, what foul dust floated in the wake of his dreams that temporarily closed out my interest in the abortive sorrows and short-winded elations of men."

> "Reserving judgements is a matter of infinite hope."

**The Short Sentence After Length:**
After building long, complex passages, Fitzgerald drops a short sentence for impact:

> "She was incurably dishonest."

> "He did not know that it was already behind him."

**The "As If" Construction (190 occurrences):**
This is Fitzgerald's signature comparative structure — using "as if" or "as though" to connect concrete observation to abstract meaning:

> "He sat down on the edge of a great table as if he were moving a checker to another square."

> "Her grey, sun-strained eyes stared straight ahead, but she had deliberately shifted our relations."

> "She sat down at the table as if she were getting into bed."

**The Question as Narrative Engine:**
328 questions in the novel. Fitzgerald uses questions to drive scenes, create tension, and let characters reveal themselves:

> "Why they came East I don't know."

> "What do you expect?"

**Sentence rule:** Build long, accumulate detail, then land short. Use em dashes for mid-sentence reversals. Use "as if" to bridge the concrete and the abstract. Let questions do the work that exposition cannot.

### Dominant Verb Categories

**Perception verbs (highest frequency):** looked, seemed, saw, watched, stared, noticed
**Movement verbs:** came, went, turned, walked, drove, passed
**Speech verbs:** said, told, asked, cried, whispered, murmured, exclaimed
**State verbs:** was, felt, knew, thought, wanted, seemed

**Verb rule:** Favor perception and movement verbs over action verbs. Characters in Fitzgerald don't fight or build — they look, turn, walk, and seem. The world is observed more than it is acted upon.

### Distinctive Adjectives

| Category | Words |
|----------|-------|
| Sensory/atmospheric | bright, dark, dim, faint, warm, cold, silent, quiet |
| Emotional/social | curious, restless, solemn, strange, desolate, romantic |

**Adjective rule:** Prefer sensory adjectives (bright, dark, cold, warm) over evaluative ones. Let the scene carry the emotion; don't tell the reader how to feel.

### Recommended Word Bank

**For atmosphere:** light, dark, bright, dim, warm, cold, grey, gold, green, silent, quiet, faint, vast, enormous
**For character:** restless, curious, solemn, strange, romantic, gorgeous, contemptuous, incredulous
**For action:** looked, seemed, turned, came, went, stood, sat, walked, drove, reached, leaned
**For speech:** said, told, asked, cried, whispered, murmured, exclaimed, insisted, protested

### Prohibited Word Bank

**Avoid:** happy, sad, beautiful, ugly (too direct — use sensory detail instead)
**Avoid:** suddenly, immediately, quickly (Fitzgerald's pace is measured, not frantic)
**Avoid:** very, really, extremely (Fitzgerald uses precise words, not intensifiers)
**Avoid:** modern slang, contemporary references, casual register

## 8. Emotion Layer (情绪层)

### Quantitative Profile

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Melancholy/loss words | 221 |
| Glamour/beauty words | 53 |
| Ratio | 4.2:1 melancholy to glamour |

Fitzgerald's emotional register is overwhelmingly elegiac — even the beauty is shot through with loss. The glamour exists to be mourned.

### Emotional Strategy

**1. Restraint over expression:**
Fitzgerald never says "Gatsby was sad." He says:

> "He stretched out his arms toward the dark water in a curious way, and, far as I was from him, I could have sworn he was trembling. Involuntarily I glanced seaward — and distinguished nothing except a single green light, minute and far away, that might have been the end of a dock."

The emotion lives in the gesture (stretching, trembling), the distance (far as I was), and the symbol (the green light). Never in direct statement.

**2. Beauty as vehicle for loss:**
Every gorgeous description in Fitzgerald is also a description of something passing or already gone:

> "The wind had blown off, leaving a loud, bright night, and the entrepreneurs and their victims and the jazz musicians and the dancers and the lights and the cars and the voices merged into one."

This is beautiful — and it describes a party that is already a memory.

**3. The devastating understatement:**
After the most violent scene in the novel (Myrtle's death), Fitzgerald gives us:

> "The 'God damned coward!' he whimpered. 'He didn't even stop his car.'"

Then:

> "The Buchanans' house floated suddenly toward us through the dark rustling trees."

The emotional pivot from horror to pastoral beauty is characteristic. The world goes on; the prose returns to its sensory mode; the devastation is in the gap.

**4. The "for a moment" technique (33 occurrences):**
Fitzgerald frequently uses "for a moment" to mark fleeting emotional states that the narrator can barely hold onto:

> "For a moment he looked at me as if he failed to understand."

> "For a moment I thought I loved her."

These micro-emotions flash and disappear, creating a sense of impermanence that pervades the novel.

### Emotional Arc

The novel's emotional trajectory is: **wonder → disillusionment → devastation → elegy.**

- Opening: Curiosity and attraction (Nick drawn to Gatsby's parties)
- Middle: Romantic hope (the reunion with Daisy)
- Crisis: Horror and moral clarity (the confrontation, Myrtle's death)
- Ending: Grief universalized into philosophy ("So we beat on...")

**Emotion rule:** Never state emotion directly. Let gesture, symbol, and sensory detail carry the feeling. Beauty and loss must coexist in every description. End scenes on understatement, not on emotional peaks.

These function as the novel's "golden sentences" — lines that circulate independently:

> "So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past."

**Transmission rule:** Every chapter should contain at least one sentence that can stand alone as a quotation — a sentence that compresses an entire theme into a single rhythmic phrase. This requires both compression and music.

## Core Writing Rules (Transferable)

1. **Open with a moral frame.** Establish the narrator's character and limitations before the story begins. The first sentence should hint at the themes without announcing them.

2. **Use a participant-observer narrator.** Someone inside the world who claims to be objective but isn't. The gap between claimed neutrality and actual judgment is where the novel's moral complexity lives.

3. **Build long, land short.** Accumulate sensory detail in long, complex sentences, then deliver key moments in short, devastating ones. "She was incurably dishonest." Five words. Total impact.

4. **Saturate with sensory detail.** Every scene must have light, color, temperature, sound. "A breeze stirred the grey haze of Daisy's fur collar." Not "the weather was cold."

5. **Use "as if" to bridge concrete and abstract.** This is the Fitzgerald connector: observe something physical, then use "as if" to reach toward its meaning. "As if he were related to one of those intricate machines that register earthquakes ten thousand miles away."

6. **Make every symbol serve double duty.** The green light is both a literal light on a dock and the American Dream. Eyes are both a character's eyes and the eyes of God. White is both a dress color and a moral judgment.

7. **Withhold information strategically.** Don't explain characters — let them reveal themselves through dialogue, gesture, and rumor. The reader should be slightly confused, slightly curious, and always reaching.

8. **Catalogue for social texture.** Long lists of names and social details create the texture of a world. But each name should carry a tiny judgment or observation — not mere enumeration.

9. **Use em dashes for mid-sentence reversals.** The em dash allows you to qualify, contradict, or deepen a statement without starting a new sentence. Fitzgerald uses it to layer meaning.

10. **Dialogue should reveal character, not advance plot.** Let characters speak in ways that expose their values, class, and self-deception. Tom's bluster, Daisy's performative charm, Gatsby's "old sport" — each voice is a moral portrait.

11. **End on rhythm, not explanation.** The final sentence must be musical and metaphorical. It should compress the entire novel's theme into a single image. "So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past."

12. **Move from the particular to the universal.** Start with a specific character and a specific story. End with a statement about all of us. This is how a novel about a bootlegger becomes a novel about America.

13. **Let beauty carry grief.** Every beautiful description should also be a description of loss. The party is gorgeous — and it's already a memory. The mansion is magnificent — and it's already empty.

14. **Use the "for a moment" technique.** Brief, fleeting emotional states that flash and disappear. This creates impermanence and the sense that feelings, like everything else, are passing.

15. **Reserve your moral judgments for the end.** Don't editorialize in the middle. Let the structure do the work. When the devastating judgment finally comes ("They were careless people"), it hits harder because it was earned through hundreds of pages of restraint.

## Anti-Rules (What NOT to Do)

1. **Do not use modern slang or casual register.** Fitzgerald's prose is formal, musical, and precise. No contractions in narrative prose (dialogue excepted), no colloquialisms, no informality.

2. **Do not state emotions directly.** Never "he was sad," "she was angry," "I felt disgusted." Always show through gesture, symbol, and sensory detail.

3. **Do not use short, choppy sentences throughout.** Fitzgerald's power comes from the contrast between long accumulation and short impact. All short sentences = monotone.

4. **Do not explain symbols.** If you have to tell the reader what the green light means, you've failed. The symbol must work on its literal level first.

5. **Do not make the narrator neutral.** A truly neutral narrator is boring. The narrator must have opinions, biases, and self-deceptions that the reader can detect.

6. **Do not resolve the ending.** Fitzgerald never provides closure. The ending must open outward, not close inward. Leave the reader with an image, not an answer.

7. **Do not use clichés or stock phrases.** Every description must be freshly perceived. "Her eyes sparkled" is forbidden. "Her grey, sun-strained eyes stared straight ahead" is the Fitzgerald way.

8. **Do not rush the descriptive passages.** The luxury of the prose is the point. If you cut the sensory detail to "get to the plot," you've destroyed the novel's reason for existing.

9. **Do not flatten the social texture.** Fitzgerald's world has layers — old money, new money, no money, aspirants, failures. Every character exists in a specific social position, and the prose must reflect that.

10. **Do not imitate Fitzgerald's specific images.** The green light, the eyes of Dr. Eckleburg, the boats against the current — these are Fitzgerald's. Extract the technique (symbolic object, recurring motif, final metaphor), not the specific images.

## Style Fingerprint

Fitzgerald's prose is **lyrical realism** — the world rendered in precise sensory detail, but with a musical, almost poetic rhythm that elevates observation into elegy. The narrator is always slightly outside the world he describes, watching with a mixture of fascination and moral unease. The sentences are long, complex, and architecturally sound, building toward moments of devastating compression. The imagery is symbolic — every detail carries both literal and metaphorical weight. The emotional register is melancholy shot through with beauty, or beauty shot through with melancholy — they are inseparable. The endings are the most famous in American literature because they refuse to resolve, instead opening outward into universal human experience.

**In one sentence:** Write the party as if you're already mourning it.
